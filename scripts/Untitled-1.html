Executive Summary: While overall progress appears high at 74.7%, this figure is critically misleading. High completion rates in Engineering (93.1%) and Procurement (95.8%) are masking a catastrophic failure in Construction (52.6%) and a near-total collapse in Commissioning (0.4%). The project is not merely delayed; it is functionally stalled at the physical implementation stage. The 2025 completion target is unachievable under any scenario.

The Illusion of Progress: EPC Breakdown

The project's health cannot be judged by the total progress number. A closer look at the Engineering, Procurement, and Construction (EPC) components reveals that while the 'paperwork' phases are nearly done, the critical 'building' phase has failed, creating an insurmountable bottleneck.

Discipline Breakdown: The Core of the Failure

The project's paralysis stems from the collapse of critical construction disciplines. Without progress in these key areas, especially Electrical and Piping, no amount of completed engineering or procurement can move the project forward. Commissioning cannot begin without completed construction.

Warehouse Gridlock: Materials Awaiting Installation

Procurement is 95.8% complete, but this success has created a new problem: a logistical nightmare. Over 4,000 critical components from key suppliers like Hitachi and Siemens are sitting idle in warehouses, unable to be installed due to the construction standstill. This represents a massive waste of resources and capital.

Risk Cascade: The Path to Regulatory Failure

The project delays are creating a domino effect of risk. Stalled construction prevents commissioning, which in turn makes it impossible to prepare for and obtain mandatory approvals from regulatory bodies like MOEI and FANR. This unpreparedness guarantees significant future delays, even if construction were to resume today.

New Forecast: A Realistic Path to Completion

Based on current progress rates, external benchmarks, and unresolved risks, a new timeline is necessary. The most optimistic completion is late 2025, but a realistic forecast places project handover between late 2026 and mid-2027. This must become the new baseline for all planning.
